ABSTRACT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This document is the slide-by-slide companion to the fourth and final session of
the Harvard HDSI "Generative AI for Scholarship" workshop series. Where the
first three sessions introduced participants to conversational assistants
(Session 1), notebook-embedded APIs (Session 2), and the Claude Code
command-line interface as an agentic coder (Session 3), this session addresses
the question that naturally follows: once an agent can read files, write code,
run scripts, and iterate on errors, how do we make it follow OUR methodology
instead of whatever statistical folklore it absorbed during pre-training?

We argue that the answer is a class of artefact that the community has
converged on under the name "skills": opinionated, version-controlled
markdown documents that specify the steps, guardrails, and failure modes of a
domain workflow, and that a coding agent must consult before acting. A skill is
neither a prompt nor a tool nor a function; it is a methodology encoded as
text, read on demand, composable with other skills, and portable across agent
runtimes that implement the emerging Agent Skills Specification.

The companion develops this argument in six movements. Section 1 positions the
session within the workshop series and the broader AI coding landscape.
Section 2 builds agents from first principles, showing that the celebrated
"agentic loop" is, literally, a while loop whose body is an LLM call plus a
tool call plus a memory update. Section 3 defines skills and distinguishes
them from the adjacent concepts they are most frequently confused with
(prompts, CLAUDE.md global instructions, Model Context Protocol tools, and
subagents). Section 4 presents four worked case studies --- a Bayesian
workflow skill, a causal inference skill, a pair of software-engineering
skills (TDD and systematic debugging), and a math-olympiad skill --- and
reports the quantitative evaluations that justify each design choice.
Section 5 translates the theory into practice: how to author a SKILL.md file,
how to test it, and how to iterate on it when it fails. Section 6 is the
hands-on exercise students completed in room B103, reproduced here so readers
can complete it at home. Section 7 is a short discussion and reading list.

The thesis of the document, stated once here in unvarnished form, is this:
coding agents in 2026 are already capable of executing almost any technical
task a graduate student can articulate. What they lack is discipline. A skill
is a vehicle for discipline. Writing one is the most cost-effective thing a
research group can do to make AI-assisted science reliable, reproducible, and
honest about its own uncertainty.

COMMENTARY

The stated title uses the word "faster", and that word is the first place a
careful reader should push back. Speed is what sells tools; it is not what
justifies them in science. If an agent produces a plausible-looking analysis
in ten seconds but skips the diagnostic checks that would have revealed the
model was misspecified, the agent has not made anyone faster; it has made
the research less defensible while hiding the cost. The session's real topic
is *better*, not *faster*, and the arc of the ninety minutes is designed to
earn that reframing.

We keep the word "faster" in the title anyway because it is what most
researchers come in looking for. Meeting audiences where they are is an
instructional choice, not a concession. By Slide 12 we will have shown that
the same agent that produces a fast analysis will also, unprompted, skip prior
predictive checks on a Bayesian model and jump to estimation on a causal
model without drawing a DAG. By Slide 15 we will have named that behavior the
*problem that skills solve*.

SLIDE 2 — WHERE WE ARE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    2/34
    Where We Are
    •  Session 1: The Basics — Gemini, NotebookLM, Gems
    •  Session 2: The AI-Empowered Coder — Colab + JupyterLab + APIs
    •  Session 3: Claude Code CLI — agentic coding from the terminal

    "In Session 3, you talked to Claude Code. You gave it a prompt, it wrote
     code, you iterated. That was a conversation. Today we go deeper."

COMMENTARY

The series has taken participants progressively closer to the metal. Session 1
lived entirely in the browser: a researcher types a question into a chat box,
a language model answers, the session is ephemeral. Session 2 put a
programmable wrapper around the same models --- the Python SDK, a Jupyter
kernel --- so that prompts could be composed, batched, and integrated with
data that lives on disk. Session 3 handed the researcher a terminal agent
that can read files, write files, and execute shell commands on its own
judgement, under human supervision, in the context of a real project
directory.

The rhetorical distinction between "conversation" and "methodology" is the
load-bearing idea of the session. A conversation is a sequence of prompts
and replies in which the human supplies the discipline each turn: you notice
that the agent skipped a step, you tell it to go back, it complies, and next
time the same conversation begins from zero. A methodology is a persistent
artefact: a document, read by the agent before it acts, that says what the
steps are, what order they go in, which ones are mandatory, and what counts
as evidence that they have been completed. Methodology trades the flexibility
of ad-hoc correction for the reliability of written procedure, and for most
scientific work this is the correct trade.

This framing has a lineage outside AI. It is the same argument that drives
standard operating procedures in wet labs, pre-analysis plans in experimental
economics, and PRISMA checklists in systematic reviews. In each case a
community that cared about reproducibility decided that the discipline of
writing the protocol down was worth the overhead of writing the protocol
down. Skills are the translation of that insight into the language agents
can read.

SLIDE 3 — THE AI CODING LANDSCAPE IN 60 SECONDS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    3/34
    The AI Coding Landscape in 60 Seconds
    •  Browser Tools (everyone): Lovable, Replit, v0, Google AI Studio
    •  IDE Agents (professional humans): Cursor, VS Code + Copilot, Windsurf
    •  CLI Agents (humans + agents): Claude Code, Codex CLI, Gemini CLI
    You already know the CLI layer from Session 3.
    Today: what makes agents actually useful for research — and how skills
    change everything.

COMMENTARY

The three layers are distinguished not only by the interface but by WHO is
presumed to be at the keyboard. Browser tools assume a user who wants to
build something and does not want to think about the shell, a filesystem, a
package manager, or a test runner. The tool takes the prompt, produces a
running artefact, and hides the rest. The model of work is "tell me what you
want and I will build it." This is extraordinarily powerful for prototyping
and for the large population of researchers who are not software engineers,
but the opacity is the point: when something goes wrong, the user has limited
surface area to diagnose the failure.

IDE agents are the middle layer. They sit inside the editor a professional
developer already uses --- VS Code, JetBrains, Cursor's Code fork --- and
they participate in the edit loop: suggesting completions, proposing
refactors, explaining code, answering questions about the local project.
The assumption is that the user can read the output, knows what a diff is,
and will accept or reject suggestions individually. The loop is still
primarily human-driven.

CLI agents are the third layer and they differ from the other two in a
subtle but consequential way: they are designed to be driven not only by
humans but by other agents. Because the CLI is text in and text out, because
each invocation is a reproducible shell command, and because the output is a
log file rather than a GUI event stream, CLI agents compose. Claude Code can
spawn a subagent to investigate a bug; that subagent can call Claude Code
again to run a test; the test output can be piped into yet another agent
that writes a summary. The CLI is the substrate on which multi-agent systems
are actually built in 2026, and it is the layer we spend the rest of the
session on.

The final sentence of the slide is a promise: today we answer "what makes
agents actually useful for research." The answer, developed across the next
thirty slides, is that agents become useful for research when they stop
being general-purpose code writers and start being domain-specific
methodology executors --- and skills are the mechanism of the transition.

SLIDE 5 — SECTION DIVIDER
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    5/34
    Agents from First Principles
    What is an agent, really?

COMMENTARY

The phrase "what is an agent, really?" is a deliberate provocation against
the word's inflation in 2025–2026 marketing. In the strictest form we care
about, an agent is a computer program that uses a language model as its
planner, is permitted to call tools that affect the world, observes the
results of those calls, and iterates until a goal is reached or until it
decides it cannot proceed. Anything less (no tool calls, no iteration) is a
chatbot. Anything more (no LLM in the loop) is a workflow. The next several
slides will make this definition concrete.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 6 — CHATBOT VS. AGENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    6/34
    Chatbot vs. Agent
    Chatbot
        → Reactive
        → Single-turn
        → No tools
    Agent
        → Proactive
        → Multi-step
        → Uses tools
    An agent is a system that uses an LLM to pursue a goal by planning and
    taking actions with tools, iterating in a feedback loop until it's done.

COMMENTARY

The three-property contrast --- reactive vs. proactive, single-turn vs.
multi-step, no tools vs. tools --- is the shortest operational definition
that still survives contact with practice. Each property is load-bearing.

*Reactive vs. proactive.* A chatbot only responds when prompted; an agent
can decide that a sub-goal is needed and pursue it without asking. In
practice this means that when you ask an agent to debug a failing test, it
will (if it chooses) open the test file, open the source file, read both,
run the test to see the actual error, grep for related code, and only then
propose a fix. A chatbot would hand the work back to you at each step.

*Single-turn vs. multi-step.* A chatbot reasons within one response; an
agent chains responses, with each step's output becoming the next step's
input. This is the mechanical source of agent power: the model's limited
context window is not the end of its reasoning horizon, because each loop
iteration compresses the previous step into an observation.

*No tools vs. tools.* A chatbot's only output is text; an agent's output
can be a file written, a command executed, an API called. This is also the
source of agent danger: a chatbot that hallucinates a command does no harm,
while an agent that hallucinates a command runs it.

The formal definition at the bottom of the slide is the one we return to
throughout the session. Each phrase in it is doing work: "uses an LLM"
(there is a language model in the planning loop, not just a script); "to
pursue a goal" (there is a goal, supplied by the human, not an infinite
dialogue); "by planning and taking actions with tools" (reasoning plus
effect); "iterating in a feedback loop" (the loop is the thing, not any
individual action); "until it's done" (there is a termination condition,
and the agent is the one who judges whether it has been met).

SLIDE 7 — THE AGENT LOOP IS A WHILE LOOP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    7/34
    The Agent Loop — It's Just a While Loop

        while not done:
            # 1. Think: what should I do next?
            plan = llm.think(goal, memory, observations)
            # 2. Act: use a tool
            result = use_tool(plan.next_action())
            # 3. Observe: what happened?
            memory.add(result)
            # 4. Decide: am I done?
            done = plan.is_complete()

    That's it. Every agent — from Claude Code to AutoGPT — is this loop.

COMMENTARY

The pseudocode on this slide is not a simplification that hides complexity
underneath; it is a faithful description of the control flow of every modern
coding agent. Simon Willison's widely cited 2025 essay "Agents" makes the
same reduction in one line --- an agent is "an LLM in a loop with tools" ---
and the pseudocode above is that line unrolled. Claude Code, Codex CLI,
Gemini CLI, AutoGPT, ReAct, Devin, and the various research prototypes
that have generated a decade of papers all have this loop somewhere at
their core. What differs between systems is not
the shape of the loop but the quality of each component: how good the LLM's
planning is, how rich the tool set is, how the memory is managed, and what
termination conditions are available.

The four-stage decomposition --- Think, Act, Observe, Decide --- is
pedagogically useful because each stage maps to a failure mode researchers
have likely already encountered. Think failures look like bad plans: the
agent decides to debug by adding print statements when it should be reading
the stack trace. Act failures look like tool misuse: the agent reaches for a
hammer when it needed a screwdriver. Observe failures look like ignored
evidence: the test output says "AssertionError on line 42" and the agent
proceeds as if it had passed. Decide failures look like premature stopping
(the agent claims success when three of six tasks are incomplete) or
infinite looping (the agent keeps modifying the same file without progress).

When we later argue that skills inject "mandatory checkpoints" into an
agent's workflow, we are really arguing that skills add a constraint to the
Think stage: the plan cannot exit a phase until a specific Observe has been
performed and logged. The loop is the substrate; the skill is the
discipline that runs on top of it.

SLIDE 8 — THE FOUR COMPONENTS OF AN AGENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    8/34
    The Four Components of an Agent
    •  LLM (the brain) — reasoning, planning, deciding what to do next
    •  Tools (the hands) — read files, write code, run scripts, call APIs
    •  Memory (the notebook) — conversation history, project context,
       learned facts
    •  Planning (the strategy) — breaking goals into steps, sequencing actions
    For researchers: the difference between asking a colleague a question vs.
    hiring a research assistant who can access your data, run your scripts,
    and follow your methodology.

COMMENTARY

The four-component decomposition is the level of abstraction at which it is
most useful to reason about agent design. Each component can be swapped
independently.

*LLM.* The planner can be any sufficiently capable language model: Claude
Opus, Claude Sonnet, GPT-4 class, Gemini, open-weight Llama and Qwen
variants. In 2026 the frontier models differ measurably in their planning
quality, particularly in long-horizon tasks, but the loop itself is
model-agnostic.

*Tools.* Tools are the surface through which the agent affects the world.
In Claude Code the core tool set is small and composable: Read, Write,
Edit, Bash, Glob, Grep, WebFetch, and the Task / Agent dispatcher that
spawns a subagent. Together these tools are enough to do every common
software-engineering task, precisely because they were chosen to cover
the primitives, not the applications. An MCP (Model Context Protocol)
server extends the tool set with domain tools: a Postgres MCP adds SQL
execution, a Slack MCP adds messaging, and so on. Tools are the level at
which the agent's capabilities are fundamentally bounded.

*Memory.* Memory is the state the agent carries across loop iterations. In
the most basic form it is the conversation history. More sophisticated
agents distinguish short-term memory (this turn's observations), medium-term
memory (the current task's accumulated context), and long-term memory
(facts persisted across sessions, typically via a memory file or a vector
store). Claude Code's memory layer is a combination of the rolling
conversation, the project-local CLAUDE.md, and the auto-memory system that
writes summaries to disk.

*Planning.* Planning is the explicit or implicit decomposition of a goal
into steps. Implicit planning happens inside the LLM's forward pass:
"I should first read the file, then search for the function, then propose
an edit." Explicit planning elevates this decomposition into a first-class
object: Claude Code's Plan mode (Shift+Tab in the terminal) asks the model
to produce a plan that the user must approve before any tool is called.
Explicit planning is strictly safer; implicit planning is faster. The right
trade depends on reversibility: if a step writes to a production database,
explicit is the only acceptable choice.

The analogy on the last line of the slide --- colleague vs. research
assistant --- is worth dwelling on. When a researcher asks a colleague a
question, the colleague does not read the data, run the scripts, or follow
the methodology; the colleague gives advice that the researcher must
interpret and apply. An agent with the four components above is not a
colleague; it is a research assistant with keys to the filesystem. The
appropriate amount of trust to place in such an assistant depends almost
entirely on whether they have been taught the lab's methodology --- that
is, whether they have been given the skills.

SLIDE 9 — WORKFLOWS VS. AGENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    9/34
    Workflows vs. Agents
    Workflow
        → Predefined code paths
        → Deterministic
        → You write the control flow
        → Like a data pipeline
        → Reliable but rigid
    Agent
        → LLM decides what's next
        → Dynamic, adaptive
        → Agent chooses the path
        → Like a research assistant
        → Flexible but undirected
    Key insight: most research tasks need something in between.
    You want flexibility, but you also want the agent to follow your
    methodology. That's where skills come in.

COMMENTARY

The workflow-vs-agent distinction is the most underappreciated axis in the
contemporary discourse about "agentic AI". A workflow is deterministic: a
Python script, a Snakemake pipeline, a DAG in Airflow. Given the same input
it produces the same output, forever. An agent is non-deterministic: the
LLM's planning step may, legitimately, choose a different path on different
runs, and this freedom is the source of its ability to handle inputs the
author never anticipated.

Both extremes fail for research workflows. Pure workflows fail because
research is full of decisions a priori unknown to the author: which
transformation the specific dataset needs, which family of models to try,
which diagnostic to run when the residuals are heavy-tailed. Pure agents
fail because research is full of procedural discipline the agent does not
know to apply: prior predictive checks before sampling, DAG construction
before estimation, holdout validation before reporting. The Bayesian
workflow and the causal inference workflow are two crystalline examples of
this pattern; we treat them in detail in Section 4.

Skills are the "something in between." A skill is a document that
fixes the *structure* of the workflow --- which steps exist, in what
order, with which mandatory checkpoints --- while leaving the *content*
of each step to the agent's LLM-driven judgement. The agent still
chooses which model family to try, which regularization to use, how to
code the covariates; the skill dictates that before any of those
choices can produce a result, the prior predictive checks must have
passed. The hybrid tends to outperform both extremes on research
tasks: pure workflows cannot adapt to unexpected inputs, and pure
agents cannot be trusted to impose procedural discipline on themselves.

Anthropic's own framing of this distinction, in their "Building Effective
Agents" essay, uses the same dichotomy and recommends that agent designers
identify the minimal flexibility their task actually requires and fix
everything else. Skills are the natural artefact of this recommendation.

SLIDE 10 — CLAUDE CODE IS AN AGENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    10/34
    Claude Code IS an Agent
    •  LLM → Claude (Sonnet, Opus)
    •  Tools → Read, Write, Edit, Bash, Glob, Grep, WebFetch, Agent...
    •  Memory → Conversation context + CLAUDE.md + auto-memory
    •  Planning → Plan mode (Shift+Tab), task tracking
    Subagents: Claude Code can spawn isolated sub-agents for parallel work.
    Each subagent gets its own context, tools, and can work independently.
    → Delegation of complex tasks in an isolated environment.

COMMENTARY

This slide instantiates the four-component decomposition from Slide 8 in a
specific system participants already know. The point of the slide is that
Claude Code is not a special or esoteric agent architecture; it is the same
while loop with specific choices made at each slot.

The subagent mechanism deserves particular attention. A subagent in Claude
Code is a new instance of the agent loop, spawned by the parent agent, with
its own context window, its own tool permissions, and its own goal. The
subagent runs to completion and returns a single message back to the parent.
This is the computational analog of delegation: the parent does not need to
know how the subagent solved the problem, only what it reported. Three
important consequences follow.

First, subagents protect the parent's context window. Research tasks are
long and the context limit is finite; if a subagent can search a large
codebase and return a 200-word summary, the parent has used 200 words of
its context where it would otherwise have used 20,000.

Second, subagents enable parallelism. If four independent sub-problems
can be solved in parallel, four subagents can be spawned simultaneously,
and the wall-clock time of the whole task drops. The math-olympiad skill
in Section 4 exploits this aggressively, spawning eight to twelve parallel
solvers.

Third, subagents enable isolation of failure. If a subagent is pursuing a
risky strategy that might go off the rails, the parent can spawn it in a
worktree (a separate git worktree of the project) so that whatever it
writes cannot contaminate the parent's working copy. This is the agent
version of sandboxing.

Subagents are what turn a single-agent loop into a multi-agent system, and
multi-agent systems are what turn skills from instructions-for-one-agent
into orchestration-primitives-for-many.

SLIDE 11 — LIVE DEMO

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    11/34
    Live Demo
    Watch the agent loop in action:
    Analyze a dataset → Visualize → Iterate on errors
    [LIVE DEMO — have screenshot backup ready]

COMMENTARY

In the live session this slide was the occasion for a five-minute
demonstration in which the instructor opened a fresh Claude Code terminal
in a project directory containing a real dataset and asked the agent to
produce an exploratory data analysis with a publication-quality figure. The
goal of the demonstration was not the final figure; it was for the audience
to see, in real time, the Think / Act / Observe / Decide loop turning over.
The agent reads the file, encounters an unexpected column type, writes a
line of code, the code errors, the agent reads the error, revises the
approach, and tries again. This is the mundane reality of agent work, and
it is important to watch once without abstraction before moving on to the
discussion of skills, because the motivation for skills is precisely that
this mundane loop can go off the rails silently when no discipline is
imposed.

For a reader of this companion who did not attend the session, the
exercise is reproducible in five minutes. Install Claude Code, open it in
any directory containing a CSV, and type: "Produce an exploratory data
analysis of this file. Save a summary markdown and one figure." Watch each
tool call. Note which steps the agent chose to perform, which it skipped,
and which it performed but in an order you would not have chosen. Write
down the answers to those three questions. They are the first draft of
your skill.

SLIDE 12 — THE PROBLEM AGENTS CREATE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    12/34
    The Problem Agents Create
    •  Ask it to build a Bayesian model → skips prior predictive checks
    •  Ask it to do causal inference → jumps straight to estimation without
       a DAG
    •  Ask it to analyze data → picks arbitrary methods, forgets diagnostics
    •  Ask it to review a paper → misses systematic checks, fabricates citations
    Agents are like brilliant but untrained research assistants —
    they can do anything, but they don't know YOUR methodology.
    This is the problem skills solve.

COMMENTARY

This slide is the hinge of the session. Every claim on it is empirical and
has been measured in the public literature on skill evaluations. We take
them in turn.

*"Ask it to build a Bayesian model, it skips prior predictive checks."*
The prior predictive check --- simulating data from the prior before
conditioning on the observed data --- is the single most recommended
diagnostic in the Bayesian workflow of Gelman et al. (2020, "Bayesian
Workflow", arXiv:2011.01808). It is also the step that unscaffolded agents
omit most frequently. The reason is straightforward: the prior predictive
check consumes tokens, appears to "slow down" the workflow, and is not
required by any library to produce an output. A well-pretrained model knows
about prior predictive checks in the abstract but will not invoke them
unprompted because nothing in its training signal rewarded the invocation.

*"Ask it to do causal inference, it jumps straight to estimation."*
Causal inference without a directed acyclic graph (DAG) is the field's
paradigm mistake. In the modern framework (Pearl, Hernán and Robins, the
target-trial literature) the DAG is not a visualization aid; it is the
*only* mechanism by which the analyst can justify the identification
strategy. Without the DAG, the agent has no way to reason about whether its
chosen adjustment set blocks the relevant back-door paths. As the
learnbayesstats essay on the causal-inference skill puts it: "Most AI tools
are designed to do more, faster. The causal-inference skill is designed to
do less, carefully." An unscaffolded agent leaps directly to estimation
because estimation looks like progress. It is not.

*"Ask it to analyze data, it picks arbitrary methods, forgets diagnostics."*
An unscaffolded agent asked to fit a regression will fit a linear model
with default arguments, report a p-value and an R-squared, and move on.
There will be no residual plot, no VIF check, no influence diagnostic, no
holdout, no mention of whether the model's assumptions are plausible at
all. The agent is not incompetent; the agent does not know your lab's
criteria for when a regression is finished.

*"Ask it to review a paper, it misses systematic checks, fabricates
citations."* Citation fabrication is the most embarrassing failure mode of
unconstrained LLM agents and the one that has damaged the field's
credibility the most. It happens because the model is optimizing for a
plausible-sounding review, and plausible-sounding reviews cite prior work.
If the model does not actually remember the relevant prior work, it
confabulates. A skill that forbids uncited claims and requires every
reference to be verified against a reachable source (DOI, arXiv ID,
URL) eliminates this failure mode.

The closing line --- "they can do anything, but they don't know YOUR
methodology" --- is the problem statement for the rest of the session.
The next twenty slides are the solution.

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)

    Skills
    Teaching AI How You Think

COMMENTARY

The phrase "how you think" is not a metaphor for style. It is the claim
that the steps you take, the order you take them in, the questions you ask
before you start, the diagnostics you run before you believe a result, and
the language you use when you report it are all part of your methodology,
and that a skill is the artefact that encodes them. A skill is not a
personality transplant for your agent; it is a procedure manual for a very
specific kind of technical work.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 14 — WHAT IS A SKILL?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    14/34
    What Is a Skill?
    A structured instruction manual that teaches an agent HOW to do a
    specific task the right way.
    •  Not code. Not a function. A methodology encoded as markdown.
    •  Like a lab protocol your research assistant must follow — step by
       step, with checkpoints.
    One-off prompt  →  Custom command  →  CLAUDE.md rules  →  SKILL  →  Subagent
    "do X"             saved prompt        always active       on-demand    delegated
    one-shot           reusable text       every session       workflow     isolated

COMMENTARY

The definition in the first line is carefully chosen. A skill is a
*structured* instruction manual --- with named sections, fixed order, and
explicit checkpoints --- not a paragraph of free-form advice. It teaches an
agent HOW to do a task, not WHAT task to do; the task is supplied by the
human at invocation time. And it teaches the task the *right* way ---
where "right" is defined by the skill's author and their community, not by
any universal standard. A different lab with different conventions would
write a different skill for the same task, and both skills could be
correct in their own context.

The bullet points distinguish a skill from adjacent artefacts. It is not
code (code cannot capture reasoning steps; a skill can say "confirm the
DAG with the user before estimating", which no function signature can
express). It is not a function (it has no inputs and outputs in a formal
sense; it is invoked by an agent that is already in a reasoning loop). It
is markdown, because markdown is what both humans and LLMs can read with
minimal friction.

The evolutionary chain on the third line --- prompt → command → CLAUDE.md
→ skill → subagent --- is the most compact explanation of where skills
fit in the larger taxonomy.

A one-off prompt is what you type into the chat box today for a task you
will not repeat. It is instantaneous, unversioned, and forgotten after the
session. A custom command (Claude Code's slash-command mechanism) is a
prompt you save to disk because you will reuse it; it is a text file that
is inserted into the conversation when the user types `/command-name`.
CLAUDE.md rules are instructions that are loaded into every session in a
given directory, regardless of what the user asks; they are the agent's
"always active" context, and they are appropriate for things like "use
snake_case" or "never write to the database without confirmation." A
skill is a step up in structure: it is loaded only when relevant,
contains a full workflow, and is designed to be consulted repeatedly
during a task rather than scanned once at the start. A subagent is a
step up in isolation: it is a new agent loop with its own context,
invoked by the parent, and returned as a single message.

The point of arranging these artefacts in a chain is that they are not
competitors; they are a progression. A workflow you invoke once belongs
as a prompt. A workflow you invoke every week belongs as a slash
command. A convention that applies to every piece of work in a
directory belongs in CLAUDE.md. A methodology with steps, checkpoints,
and guardrails belongs as a skill. A task that should run in isolation
with its own context belongs as a subagent, and subagents themselves
often have skills attached.

*A bridge from Session 3.* Participants who attended Chris Stubbs's
Session 3 ("Unleashing Claude Code CLI as a Problem Solver") completed
an exercise called "Building Custom Skills in Claude Code" in which
the outcome was reusable slash commands. Those are the "Custom
command" step on the chain above --- saved prompts invoked by typing
`/name`, and already a real improvement over retyping the same
instructions each session. What Session 4 calls a "skill" is the next
step on the chain: a directory containing a SKILL.md with structured
frontmatter, an ordered workflow, critical rules, gotchas, and
optional references/ and scripts/ subdirectories. The word "skill"
is overloaded between the two sessions, and the distinction is not
rhetorical: a slash command inserts text into the conversation once,
while a SKILL.md is read and re-consulted by the agent as it works
through a task. If you walked out of Session 3 thinking "I've already
done skills", today is the step where skills stop being saved prompts
and start being methodology.

SLIDE 15 — WHY SKILLS MATTER FOR RESEARCH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    15/34
    Why Skills Matter for Research
    Without Skills
        → Agent guesses your methodology
        → No validation gates
        → Different results each time
        → Generic, surface-level output
        → Skips what it doesn't know
    With Skills
        → Agent follows YOUR methodology
        → Mandatory checkpoints
        → Consistent, reproducible workflow
        → Domain-appropriate output
        → Encodes community knowledge
    "Contains critical guardrails that agents won't apply unprompted."

COMMENTARY

The two columns of this slide are not a before/after advertisement; they
are testable claims. Each bullet in the left column can be verified by
running a prompt on an unconfigured agent and observing the failure, and
each bullet in the right column can be verified by running the same prompt
on an agent with the appropriate skill installed. The case studies in
Section 4 are exactly these experiments.

*Agent guesses your methodology vs. agent follows your methodology.* The
unconfigured agent selects from the distribution of methodologies in its
training data, weighted by how frequently they appear and how clearly they
were labelled. This distribution is dominated by tutorial blog posts and
undergraduate textbooks, which rarely exemplify the best practice for any
given subfield. The configured agent reads a document that says "in this
lab, for this task, the methodology is X", and X wins.

*No validation gates vs. mandatory checkpoints.* The most important
structural feature of a skill is the checkpoint --- the instruction that
says "before proceeding to step N+1, verify that step N has been
completed." Checkpoints are the mechanism by which skills prevent the
most common failure mode of agents: rushing past a diagnostic because the
diagnostic's role is not visible in the immediate goal. The Bayesian
skill's prior predictive check is a checkpoint. The causal inference
skill's DAG confirmation is a checkpoint. The TDD skill's failing test
is a checkpoint.

*Different results each time vs. consistent, reproducible workflow.* An
unconfigured agent run twice on the same task produces different
workflows because the LLM's sampling is non-deterministic and because the
model's implicit prior over methodologies is broad. A configured agent
produces the same workflow because the skill fixes it; only the content
of each step varies with the input.

*Generic, surface-level output vs. domain-appropriate output.* "Generic"
means output that would be acceptable in a textbook but is not
publishable in a field journal. Bayesian output that reports a 95%
credible interval when the field convention is 94% HDI; causal output
that reports a point estimate without a refutation test; software
output that ships without tests. The skill encodes the field's
publication standards and produces output at that standard by default.

*Skips what it doesn't know vs. encodes community knowledge.* This is
the most consequential difference. The unscaffolded agent cannot know
what it does not know, because nothing in the task description tells
it. The skill, written by someone who has made the mistakes and
documented the gotchas, is the externalization of that community
knowledge and the mechanism by which it is transferred to the agent.

The closing quote --- "contains critical guardrails that agents won't
apply unprompted" --- is from the baygent-skills project README and
captures the empirical heart of the argument: the observation is not
theoretical, it is measured, and the measurement is reproducible.

SLIDE 16 — SKILLS VS. EVERYTHING ELSE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    16/34
    Skills vs. Everything Else
    •  Skills vs Prompts: Reusable file on disk vs. one-off text you type
    •  Skills vs CLAUDE.md: Invoked on demand vs. always active (lab protocol
       vs. dress code)
    •  Skills vs MCP: Workflow instructions vs. tool connectivity (assembly
       manual vs. screwdriver)
    •  Skills vs Subagents: Methodology guidance vs. task delegation
    Skills GUIDE agents. An agent without skills is like a grad student
    without training. An agent with skills is like a grad student who has
    internalized your lab's best practices.

COMMENTARY

Each of the four contrasts on this slide is about keeping the
categories clean.

*Skills vs. prompts.* A prompt is what you type; a skill is what lives
on disk. The practical consequence is version control: a skill can be
committed to git, shared with a lab, and reviewed in a pull request; a
prompt cannot. When a lab wants to enforce a methodology across all its
members, the unit of enforcement is the skill file in the shared
repository, not the prompt in each person's head.

*Skills vs. CLAUDE.md.* CLAUDE.md is loaded into every session in a
directory. Its content becomes part of the agent's permanent system
prompt for the life of the conversation. A skill is loaded on demand,
when the agent identifies that the current task matches the skill's
description. CLAUDE.md should contain only the things that apply to
every task in the project; skills should contain everything that
applies only to specific tasks. The "lab protocol vs. dress code"
analogy captures this: a dress code applies always, a lab protocol
applies when you are doing that particular experiment.

The failure mode of putting workflow instructions in CLAUDE.md is
context bloat. If CLAUDE.md contains every methodology the lab might
ever want, the agent carries all of them in every session, and the
important ones are diluted by the irrelevant ones. Skills are
load-on-demand precisely to avoid this.

*Skills vs. MCP (Model Context Protocol).* MCP is the protocol through
which agents gain new tools: a Postgres MCP server gives the agent SQL
execution; a GitHub MCP server gives the agent pull-request access; a
Slack MCP server gives the agent messaging. MCP expands the set of
*actions* the agent can take. Skills expand the set of *workflows*
the agent can follow. They are complementary: a well-designed research
setup has MCP servers for its data sources and tools, and skills for
its methodologies, and the skills reference the MCP tools where
appropriate. The "assembly manual vs. screwdriver" analogy is precise:
skills tell the agent how to assemble the result, MCP gives the agent
the screwdriver it needs to turn the screws.

*A bridge from Session 2.* Participants who attended Michelle
Audirac's Session 2 ("Hugging Face Workshop") worked through her
Section 2, "Use Hugging Face MCP with Claude Code", which showed how
to expose HuggingFace models --- datasets, transformers pipelines,
SegFormer for image segmentation --- to Claude Code through the
Model Context Protocol. That session gave you the *screwdriver* half
of the analogy above: new tools in the agent's toolbelt. Today's
session gives you the *assembly manual* half: the methodology that
decides which tool to reach for and in what order. The two halves
are designed to click together. A realistic post-workshop setup for
a Harvard researcher looks like this: MCP servers from Session 2
(HuggingFace, plus whatever domain tools you have added since) plus
CLAUDE.md conventions from Session 3 plus skills from today, all
composed in a single Claude Code session. None of the three layers
replaces the others; each solves a different kind of problem.

*Skills vs. subagents.* A subagent is a computational primitive: a
separately-scoped agent instance invoked by its parent. A skill is a
text artefact. They are on different axes. A subagent can be given a
skill to follow; a skill can instruct the parent to spawn subagents.
The math-olympiad skill in Section 4 is an example of a skill that
orchestrates many subagents; the parent agent reads the skill, and the
skill says "spawn eight solver subagents, then spawn two verifier
subagents, then synthesize."

The grad-student analogy on the last line is the one the slide deck
returns to. A grad student without training is bright and confused; a
grad student with training is bright and disciplined. The discipline
is what makes them useful for publishable work. Skills are that
discipline, externalized.

SLIDE 17 — ANATOMY OF A SKILL.MD FILE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    17/34
    Anatomy of a SKILL.md File

    ---
    name: my-research-skill           # ← identifier & slash command
    description: >                    # ← WHEN to activate (trigger phrases)
      Opinionated workflow for X.
      Use when asked to do Y or Z.
    ---

    # Skill Name                      # ← body: the actual instructions

    ## Workflow overview              # ← sequential steps
    1. **Think** -- Define the question
    2. **Validate** -- Check assumptions   ⚠️ ASK USER
    3. **Implement** -- Write the code
    4. **Diagnose** -- Run checks

    ## Critical rules                 # ← guardrails / iron laws
    - Always do X before Y
    - Never skip step 4

    ## Common gotchas                 # ← hard-won domain knowledge
    - Library Z silently ignores flag W
    ...

    Directory structure:
    my-skill/
    ├── SKILL.md        # required
    ├── references/     # loaded on demand
    │   └── details.md
    └── scripts/        # executable tools
        └── validate.py

COMMENTARY

The SKILL.md file has exactly three parts that matter, and each part is
load-bearing.

*Frontmatter.* The YAML block between the two `---` markers contains two
fields: `name` and `description`. The name is both the identifier and
(in Claude Code) the slash-command invocation; `name: data-analysis`
means the user can type `/data-analysis` to invoke the skill
explicitly. The description is how the agent decides whether the skill
is relevant. When the user types a prompt, the agent compares the
prompt against each skill's description and activates the skill if the
description's trigger phrases match. This means the description must
contain the phrases a user would actually write: "analyze a dataset",
"review a paper", "build a Bayesian model". A description of "performs
data work" will not trigger when the user says "could you explore this
CSV for me".

Description writing is a skill in itself and the most common source of
silent skill failures. If your skill never seems to activate, the
description is almost always the problem. The fix is to enumerate the
likely phrasings and list them explicitly.

*Body.* The body of the skill is the instructions, and its structure
has converged (across baygent-skills, superpowers, the Anthropic
official skills, and the agentskills.io spec) on three sections.

The *Workflow* section is an ordered list of steps. Each step has a
name, a short imperative description, and sometimes a checkpoint
marker (`⚠️ ASK USER`, `⚠️ CONFIRM`, `⚠️ VERIFY`). The checkpoint
markers are the skill's enforcement mechanism. When the agent reads
"⚠️ CONFIRM", it knows it must halt and surface the current state to
the user before proceeding. A skill with no checkpoints is a
suggestion; a skill with checkpoints is a procedure.

The *Critical rules* section is the iron-law list. These are the
statements of the form "always do X" and "never do Y". They exist
because when the agent is under time pressure --- when the user has
asked for a quick answer, when the context is getting long, when the
LLM's attention is elsewhere --- the checkpoints can be silently
skipped. The critical rules are the backstop: even if every other
section is forgotten, the critical rules must be obeyed. In practice,
a skill with more than seven critical rules tends not to be followed
consistently; between three and five is the sweet spot.

The *Common gotchas* section is the externalization of hard-won
domain knowledge. Each entry is typically of the form "when X, then
Y, because Z" --- the failure mode, the correction, and the reason.
Gotchas are the most valuable part of a skill because they are the
knowledge that cannot be derived from any textbook; they are the
knowledge that exists only because someone made the mistake once.
When a lab adopts a skill, the gotchas section is the first thing
people add to --- every bug that took an hour to debug becomes a
gotcha entry, and subsequent agents and subsequent humans never need
to rediscover it.

*Directory structure.* A skill is typically a directory, not a
single file. The directory contains SKILL.md (required) plus
optional `references/` and `scripts/` subdirectories. The references
directory holds long supporting documents that the agent loads only
when relevant; this is the mechanism of *progressive disclosure*,
which keeps the skill's top-level instructions concise while still
providing depth when needed. The scripts directory holds executable
utilities --- validators, plotters, test runners --- that the skill
instructs the agent to invoke at specific steps. A SKILL.md that
says "run `scripts/check_convergence.py` after sampling" is strictly
more reliable than one that says "check convergence", because the
script is deterministic and its output is a pass/fail signal the
agent can act on.

The progressive-disclosure idea deserves emphasis. A skill with
everything crammed into SKILL.md is forced to choose between being
too long (and diluting the important instructions) and being too
short (and omitting the details that matter for hard cases).
Progressive disclosure lets SKILL.md be short and pointed, while
the references directory carries the depth. The agent reads the
references only when it needs them, which keeps the context window
manageable.

SLIDE 18 — EXAMPLE 1: BAYESIAN WORKFLOW SKILL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    18/34
    Example 1: Bayesian Workflow Skill
    baygent-skills · learnbayesstats.com · Workflow from Gelman et al. (2020,
    arXiv:2011.01808)
    1.  Formulate the generative story
    2.  Specify priors (documented rationale, not "Normal(0,1) and vibes")
    3.  Implement in PyMC 5+ (coords, dims, nutpie sampler)
    4.  Prior predictive checks ← MANDATORY before sampling
    5.  Inference (nutpie, reproducible descriptive seeds)
    6.  Diagnose convergence (R̂ < 1.01, ESS, divergences, E-BFMI)
    7.  Model criticism (posterior predictive + LOO-PIT calibration)
    8.  Prior sensitivity (psense_summary)
    9.  Compare models (LOO-CV, ELPD with SE, stacking weights)
    10. Report (94% HDI, no frequentist language, companion markdown)
    With skill (6/6 scenarios):     100%
    Without skill:                   90.5%
    Time cost of rigor:              +29%
    Token cost:                      +87%

COMMENTARY

*This is the "doing math faster" example.* The workshop title promises
"coding and doing math faster with LLMs and agentic workflows," and a
reader who glanced at the event page might have expected a block of
slides on symbolic proofs or computer algebra. For most researchers in
the HDSI audience the math work that an agent can actually accelerate
is statistical math: posterior inference, model comparison, sensitivity
analysis, calibration. The Bayesian workflow skill is therefore not
just "a case study of a skill"; it is the applied-math content of the
workshop, made concrete. The 90.5% → 100% numbers in the evaluation
are the "faster" claim cashed out as a measured improvement on a
statistical task, and the ten steps below are the "math" content
broken into pieces that an agent can actually execute.

The Bayesian workflow skill is the canonical example, and it is worth
taking each step seriously because each step encodes a lesson that the
field learned the hard way.

*Step 1. Formulate the generative story.* Before any code is written, the
analyst describes in prose how they imagine the data were generated: what
are the latent quantities, what are the observables, how are they
connected. This step is the Bayesian equivalent of defining the
experiment before running it, and it is skipped most often because it
feels like discussion rather than work. In the skill, it is the first
numbered step so that it cannot be skipped by construction.

*Step 2. Specify priors with documented rationale.* The phrase "not
'Normal(0,1) and vibes'" is a gentle slander of the default the
unscaffolded agent will produce. A documented rationale means: "the
prior on the coefficient is Normal(0, 2.5) because on the standardized
scale a shift of more than five standard deviations is implausible for
this biological mechanism." This is not extra work; it is the justification
that any reviewer will eventually ask for.

*Step 3. Implement in PyMC 5+ with coords, dims, and the nutpie sampler.*
This is the skill making an opinionated choice. PyMC has supported
coordinate and dimension labelling since version 3.9 (2020); the feature
matured in v4 and is standard in PyMC 5, and it dramatically improves
the interpretability of posterior summaries. The nutpie sampler (a Rust
implementation of NUTS) is substantially faster and more robust in
practice than the default Python implementation. The skill pins these
choices so that every analysis the agent produces is on the same
substrate and is comparable across runs.

*Step 4. Prior predictive checks, marked MANDATORY.* This is the most
important step in the skill because it is the step the unscaffolded
agent skips. The prior predictive check draws samples from the prior,
pushes them through the generative model, and produces synthetic data.
If the synthetic data are manifestly implausible --- negative heights,
probabilities outside [0,1], orders-of-magnitude-wrong counts --- the
prior is wrong and must be revised. This check is cheap (no MCMC is
involved), and it catches errors that would otherwise only be visible
after a day of posterior exploration.

*Step 5. Inference with nutpie and reproducible seeds.* The reproducible
seed matters because Bayesian inference is stochastic and the skill
promises reproducibility; the nutpie choice matters because NUTS with a
bad sampler will produce divergences that look like model misspecification
and waste time on a nonexistent problem.

*Step 6. Convergence diagnostics with R-hat < 1.01, ESS, divergences, and
E-BFMI.* The tighter 1.01 threshold is from Vehtari, Gelman, Simpson,
Carpenter, and Bürkner (2021, *Bayesian Analysis*), which introduced a
rank-normalized, split-chain R-hat and argued that the older 1.1 guideline
(traceable to Brooks & Gelman 1998) is too permissive for modern
practice. The effective
sample size criterion ensures that posterior summaries are not dominated
by autocorrelation. The divergence count guards against bias from the
sampler's inability to explore high-curvature regions. The E-BFMI guards
against funnel pathologies in hierarchical models. Any one of these
failing is a reason not to interpret the posterior.

*Step 7. Posterior predictive checks plus LOO-PIT calibration.* The
posterior predictive check is the complement of the prior predictive
check: after fitting, does the model reproduce features of the observed
data? LOO-PIT is a calibration measure: if the model is well calibrated,
the LOO-predicted ranks of the observations are uniform. A peaked
LOO-PIT distribution means the model is overconfident.

*Step 8. Prior sensitivity with psense_summary.* Prior sensitivity
quantifies how much the posterior moves when the prior is perturbed. If
the posterior is extremely sensitive to the prior, the data are not
informative and the conclusions are fragile. `psense_summary` is the
ArviZ utility that implements this; the skill names the tool so the
agent does not have to guess.

*Step 9. Model comparison via LOO-CV, ELPD with standard errors, and
stacking weights.* Model comparison in the skill is not "pick the one
with the highest LOO-ELPD"; it is "compare the ELPD difference to its
standard error, and if they are of the same order, prefer the simpler
model." This is the modern Bayesian model-selection norm and it is what
prevents the agent from reporting a 0.1-ELPD win as a meaningful
improvement.

*Step 10. Report with 94% HDI, no frequentist language, and a companion
markdown.* The 94% HDI (rather than 95%) is a community convention
descending from McElreath's Statistical Rethinking; it is a reminder
that the interval is a probability statement about the parameter, not
a sampling-theoretic statement about the procedure. "No frequentist
language" means no talk of p-values, Type I error, or confidence
intervals; the posterior is a probability distribution and the
report should describe it that way. The companion markdown is the
human-readable record that the analysis actually produced.

The empirical numbers on the slide come from an evaluation that
Alex Andorra (the author of the baygent-skills bayesian-workflow
skill) reported in the accompanying essay "Bayesian Workflow Agent
Skill: PyMC + ArviZ" on learnbayesstats.com (March 16, 2026). The
evaluation ran six held-out scenarios covering logistic regression,
hierarchical models, troubleshooting, count data, model comparison,
and prior elicitation. The unscaffolded agent scored 90.5% on average
against an assertion-based rubric; the skill-armed agent scored 100%
(6/6). Individual scenario scores without the skill ranged from 88%
to 100%, which is worth knowing: the 90.5% average is not the result
of a narrow miss in every scenario but of a small number of scenarios
where the unscaffolded agent missed concrete checks (skipping coords
and dims, reverting to frequentist language, failing to warn on
hidden convergence problems). The cost of the rigor was approximately
29% more wall-clock time and 87% more tokens per task. N = 6 is a
small sample; the numbers should be read as directional evidence of a
large effect rather than a precision estimate, and Appendix D
tabulates the provenance.

These numbers warrant interpretation. A reader might conclude that
90.5% vs. 100% is a small improvement and the cost is not worth it.
Two considerations push back. First, the failures in the unscaffolded
case were not distributed randomly across minor polishes; the reported
failures were of mandatory steps like prior predictive checks, and a
single such failure can invalidate the entire analysis, making the
90.5% average misleading as a "closeness" signal. Second, the 87%
token cost is paid once per analysis and is dominated by diagnostic
code that a careful analyst would have to write anyway. In that sense
the rigor is not overhead; it is part of the analysis.

Andorra's own framing of the problem, in the accompanying blog post,
is worth quoting: the code runs, the plots render, "but the *analysis*
is subtly, quietly wrong --- and that's the dangerous part. Not
syntax errors. Not crashes." The skill exists to catch the wrongness
that looks like correctness.

--- Practical example: the baygent-skills repository ---

The baygent-skills bayesian-workflow directory is the concrete
reference implementation participants in this workshop should open,
clone, and read. A full clone produces the following layout:

    bayesian-workflow/
    ├── README.md
    ├── SKILL.md             (the file the agent reads)
    ├── main.py
    ├── pyproject.toml
    ├── references/
    │   ├── priors.md
    │   ├── diagnostics.md
    │   ├── model-criticism.md
    │   ├── model-comparison.md
    │   ├── hierarchical.md
    │   ├── sensitivity.md
    │   └── reporting.md
    └── scripts/
        ├── diagnose_model.py
        └── calibration_check.py

Every design idea discussed in this companion is observable in that
directory. The frontmatter of SKILL.md declares the name and
activation triggers. The body of SKILL.md holds the ten-step
workflow, the critical rules, and the gotchas. The references/
directory is the progressive-disclosure mechanism: seven topical
markdown files that the agent loads only when the workflow step
needs them (for example, references/sensitivity.md is loaded when
the agent reaches Step 8 and needs to run prior sensitivity). The
scripts/ directory holds two executable Python utilities that the
skill instructs the agent to run as part of its workflow --- these
are the "utility scripts" bullet in Slide 19.

For a reader who has not yet written their own skill, the fastest
way to internalize the pattern is:

    git clone https://github.com/Learning-Bayesian-Statistics/baygent-skills
    cd baygent-skills/bayesian-workflow
    cat SKILL.md            # read the top-level instructions
    ls references/          # see what gets loaded on demand
    cat references/diagnostics.md
    cat scripts/diagnose_model.py

The repository is MIT licensed and is explicitly intended to be read,
forked, and adapted. A participant whose research involves any
Bayesian modelling can fork the repository, edit SKILL.md to match
their lab's conventions (different HDI width, different sampler,
different priors, different reporting template), and use the result
as their own skill. The progressive-disclosure directories should be
kept or pruned based on what depth the lab actually needs.

Several features of the current SKILL.md (v1.2, by Alex Andorra,
MIT licensed) are worth highlighting because they are less visible
in a summary than they are in the file itself:

- The convergence rule is compound: R̂ > 1.01 *or* ESS < 100 × chains
  flags an unreliable fit. The skill does not let a clean R̂ mask a
  low ESS, nor vice versa.
- Reproducible random seeds are generated from the analysis name via
  `sum(map(ord, "analysis-name"))`, so that two independent runs of
  the same analysis use the same seed by construction.
- The skill uses regularized (Finnish) horseshoe priors for
  high-dimensional regression, with `target_accept ≥ 0.95` to handle
  the resulting posterior geometry.
- A gotcha warns that nutpie silently ignores log-likelihood and
  log-prior storage; the skill instructs the agent to compute them
  explicitly post-sampling so that downstream LOO-CV and prior
  sensitivity still work.
- A gotcha warns that flat scale priors in hierarchical models
  produce funnel geometries; the skill recommends `Gamma(2, …)` or
  `Exponential` in their place.
- The critical rule "use posterior mean, never median, for predictive
  probabilities" is in the file --- this is the rule added in v1.1
  after the real-world stress test surfaced the median-as-expectation
  bug (see Slide 19 commentary below).

A participant who wants to understand how to structure a skill of
their own will get more from twenty minutes of browsing this
repository than from any template.

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    19/34
    What Makes This Skill Great
    •  Opinionated defaults: 94% HDI, nutpie sampler, reproducible seeds —
       each justified
    •  Guardrails: "Always run prior predictive checks BEFORE sampling" —
       the #1 agent violation

COMMENTARY

The five properties on the top half of the slide are a checklist for
evaluating any skill, not just this one.

*Opinionated defaults.* Every choice the skill makes is explicit and
justified. The skill does not say "choose a sampler"; it says "use
nutpie because it is faster and more robust than the default." This
is a cost: the skill is tied to the current best practice and must
be updated when the best practice changes. It is also a benefit: the
user reading the skill can see why each choice was made and is free
to override it with full information.

*Guardrails.* A guardrail is different from a rule in that it is
specifically targeted at a known failure mode. "Always run prior
predictive checks before sampling" is a guardrail because the known
failure mode is "agents skip prior predictive checks". A skill
written by someone with no experience of agent failures will not
have guardrails; a skill iterated on real agent outputs will be full
of them.

*Fifteen-plus critical rules.* The rule count is a design decision:
too few and the skill is under-specified, too many and the agent
loses the forest for the trees. Fifteen is at the high end of what
is followable; it is justifiable here because the Bayesian workflow
is unusually dense with mandatory steps. A simpler skill should have
three to five.

*Gotchas table.* "Any divergences → do NOT interpret the posterior"
is an example of a gotcha that encapsulates years of HMC folklore.
It is the kind of knowledge that a first-year graduate student gets
wrong and a fifth-year graduate student knows instinctively. The
gotcha table transfers the fifth-year knowledge to the first-year's
agent.

*Progressive disclosure with references/ and scripts/.* The SKILL.md
itself is kept short; the references directory holds longer
explanations of topics like "how to choose a prior for a variance
parameter" that are loaded only when the agent needs them; the
scripts directory holds validators that the skill invokes at
specific steps.

The "real-world stress test" section is the instructive heart of
this slide, and it is documented in Andorra's follow-up essay
"Bayesian Workflow Skill: Real-World Stress Test (v1.1)" on
learnbayesstats.com (March 23, 2026). The evaluation against six
synthetic scenarios passed at v1.0. Andorra then applied the skill to
a real problem of his own: the *Soccer Factor Model* (SFMMO), an
ordered logistic hierarchical model predicting match outcomes across
five European football leagues over roughly 10,000 matches, with
more than thirty features. Three critical bugs surfaced.

Bug 1, in Andorra's own words: "The skilled agent computed
out-of-sample predictions by taking the **median** of posterior
predictive probabilities across samples. This is wrong." Medians do
not commute with nonlinear transforms, so the median of posterior
predictive probabilities is not the probability of the outcome under
the posterior; using it breaks the coherence between the reported
point prediction and the underlying distribution. Fix in v1.1: a new
critical rule, "always use the posterior mean (not median) for
predictive probabilities", plus an instruction to use
`pm.set_data()` with `pm.sample_posterior_predictive()` rather than
hand-rolling the prediction loop.

Bug 2: for high-dimensional regression the skill lacked guidance on
sparsity priors. Andorra explicitly cites Piironen and Vehtari (2017)
on the regularized horseshoe --- this is the paper the companion
references in Appendix A. Fix in v1.1: a new section on sparsity
priors with templates for the regularized (Finnish) horseshoe, and
an accompanying critical rule that uses `target_accept ≥ 0.95` to
manage the posterior geometry it induces.

Bug 3: for ordered classification, the skill's diagnostic step did
not include per-class calibration plots or temporal out-of-sample
breakdowns. Aggregate calibration can look good while individual
classes are miscalibrated, and the SFMMO case showed exactly that.
Fix in v1.1: new classification evaluation helpers including
expected calibration error (ECE) and per-class calibration plots,
surfaced as a step in the model-criticism phase.

All three fixes landed in v1.1 within roughly a week of the stress
test (the repository is currently at v1.2). Andorra's summary of the
experience is worth quoting verbatim because it is shorter and
sharper than any paraphrase: "Evals are necessary. They're not
sufficient." Synthetic benchmarks caught what was predictable;
real-world use caught what was not. The general principle for skill
engineering is that a skill is never finished, and the feedback loop
between real-world use and skill revision is the mechanism of
long-term improvement. Every skill should have a changelog; every
changelog entry should point to the problem that motivated the
revision, and ideally to the primary source that justifies the fix.

SLIDE 20 — EXAMPLE 2: CAUSAL INFERENCE SKILL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    20/34
    Example 2: Causal Inference Skill
    baygent-skills · "The thinking-first design" · PyMC + CausalPy + DoWhy

    THINKING PHASE (no code)
        → 1. Formulate causal question
        → 2. Draw the DAG        ⚠️ CONFIRM
        → 3. Identify strategy   ⚠️ CONFIRM
        → 4. Choose design       ⚠️ CONFIRM

    DOING PHASE
        → 5. Estimate model
        → 6. Mandatory refutation tests
        → 7. Interpret with intervals
        → 8. Report assumptions first

    With skill (6/6 scenarios): 100%
    Without skill:              68%
    Gap from reasoning alone:   32 points

    Key rule: "No estimation without a confirmed DAG."
    The 32-point gap is entirely reasoning discipline.
    Skills compose — delegates PyMC work to bayesian-workflow.

COMMENTARY

The causal inference skill is the most pedagogically important of the
four case studies because it shows a skill that provides almost no
technical machinery --- every tool it uses is already in CausalPy,
DoWhy, or PyMC --- and yet produces a 32-percentage-point improvement
on a benchmark. The entire improvement comes from imposing discipline
on reasoning, and this is the clearest empirical demonstration that
the bottleneck in agent-assisted science is not tool availability but
procedural discipline.

The two-phase structure --- Thinking Phase and Doing Phase --- is the
skill's central design choice and is lifted directly from the causal
inference literature. The Thinking Phase is the part where the
analyst (human or agent) decides what question is being asked, what
assumptions are being made, and what strategy is justified by those
assumptions; the Doing Phase is where the computation happens. In
most analysts' practice these phases are interleaved, and the
interleaving is where mistakes happen: the analyst starts fitting a
model before the DAG is clear, discovers that the model does not fit
the data, adjusts the model, and in the adjustment loses track of
what assumption was originally justified. The skill enforces
separation.

*Step 1. Formulate the causal question.* A causal question has a
specific form: "what is the effect of X on Y in population P, under
intervention I?" Most research questions are stated in a vague form
("does X affect Y?") that cannot be answered without further
specification. The skill's first step is to force the specification.

*Step 2. Draw the DAG (confirmed by the user).* The DAG is a
directed acyclic graph in which nodes are variables and edges are
causal relationships hypothesized by the analyst. The DAG is not a
data summary; it is a statement of prior causal beliefs. The ⚠️ CONFIRM
marker means the agent must present the DAG to the user and obtain
explicit approval before proceeding. This is non-negotiable: the
DAG encodes the analyst's causal beliefs, and the agent is not the
analyst.

*Step 3. Identify the strategy (confirmed by the user).* Given the
DAG, multiple identification strategies may be available:
back-door adjustment, front-door adjustment, instrumental variables,
difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity. The strategy is
chosen by inspecting the DAG for the set of variables that blocks
all back-door paths from treatment to outcome. This is a formal
procedure (do-calculus) and the agent can perform it, but the human
must confirm the result because a wrong DAG produces a wrong
strategy and the wrong strategy cannot be diagnosed from data alone.

*Step 4. Choose the design (confirmed by the user).* The design is
the specific statistical model that implements the identification
strategy: a regression, a propensity-score model, a Bayesian
hierarchical model with CausalPy, a synthetic control, etc. The
agent can propose; the human confirms.

*Step 5. Estimate the model.* Only now is code written. Note that
by this point the DAG, the strategy, and the design have all been
confirmed, and the code is merely the execution of a plan that has
already been validated. The cost of writing code on a confirmed plan
is low; the cost of writing code on an unconfirmed plan is the
revision cycle.

*Step 6. Mandatory refutation tests.* Refutation tests are
experiments that would falsify the estimate if the assumptions are
wrong: placebo outcomes, negative controls, random common causes,
unobserved confounder bounds. DoWhy provides a suite of these out
of the box. The skill makes them mandatory because they are the
only mechanism by which the analysis can be distinguished from a
data-fitting exercise.

*Step 7. Interpret with intervals.* Causal effects are estimated
with uncertainty; the skill forbids point-only reporting. Intervals
may be credible (from a Bayesian model), bootstrap (from a frequentist
one), or analytical, but they must be present.

*Step 8. Report assumptions first.* The report begins with the
assumptions, not the estimate. This is the deepest philosophical
choice in the skill, and it is lifted from the causal inference
literature's gold standard. A report that begins with the estimate
invites the reader to accept the estimate as a fact; a report that
begins with the assumptions invites the reader to evaluate whether
the assumptions are plausible and, if they are, to accept the
estimate as the conclusion that follows. The ordering is a
correction to the natural incentive structure of publication, which
rewards clean estimates.

The empirical comparison is stark. Without the skill, Claude fitted
a plausible-looking regression for every question and called it
done, passing 68% of scenario checks. With the skill, the agent
refused to estimate until the DAG was drawn, and the DAG revealed in
several cases that the naive regression was biased; the skill-armed
agent passed 100%. The 32-percentage-point gap is reasoning
discipline, not technical machinery.

The note about composition --- "skills compose: delegates PyMC work
to bayesian-workflow" --- is important. A skill does not need to
re-derive everything from scratch; it can reference another skill
and delegate. The causal inference skill, at Step 5, tells the agent
to invoke the Bayesian workflow skill when the design is Bayesian.
This is the skills ecosystem starting to look like a package
ecosystem: composable, versioned, interdependent.

To paraphrase the learnbayesstats essay on which this slide is
based: the gap between a good causal analysis and a bad one is
almost never in the code; it is in the reasoning. The skill enforces
the reasoning. "Earn the right to say 'causes'" is the philosophical
claim; the eight-step workflow is the operational procedure that
earns it.

SLIDE 21 — EXAMPLE 3: SOFTWARE ENGINEERING SKILLS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    21/34
    Example 3: Software Engineering Skills
    github.com/obra/superpowers

    Test-Driven Development
        → "NO PRODUCTION CODE WITHOUT A FAILING TEST FIRST"
        → Enforces RED → GREEN → REFACTOR
        → Code before tests? Delete + redo.
        → The discipline even experts skip.

    Systematic Debugging
        → "NO FIXES WITHOUT ROOT CAUSE INVESTIGATION FIRST"
        → 4 phases: Investigate → Analyze → Hypothesize → Implement
        → 3+ failed fixes? Question the arch.

    Iron laws prevent the most common and most dangerous shortcuts.

COMMENTARY

The superpowers repository is Jesse Vincent's collection of
software-engineering skills; its two flagship entries are the TDD
skill and the systematic-debugging skill, and each is built around
a single "iron law" that the skill enforces absolutely.

*Test-Driven Development.* The iron law is "no production code
without a failing test first." The RED → GREEN → REFACTOR cycle
means: write a test that fails (RED), write the minimum production
code that makes it pass (GREEN), then refactor both the test and
the code without breaking anything (REFACTOR). TDD is the discipline
whose benefits are most celebrated in software engineering pedagogy
and least practiced in research software, because in research the
incentive to "just make it work" is immediate and the incentive to
"make it testable" is deferred. An agent that writes code without a
test first is producing exactly the code the research community
complains about when a paper's results do not reproduce.

The "code before tests? delete and redo" rule is severe on
purpose. The reason is that if the agent is allowed to write code
and then "add tests later", the tests will be written to match the
code's behavior rather than the code's specification, and they will
catch nothing. The only way for tests to be genuine is for them to
have been written before the code they constrain. This is a lesson
software engineering learned in the 2000s; the skill transfers it
to agent-written code in the 2020s.

*Systematic Debugging.* The iron law is "no fixes without root
cause investigation first." The four phases --- Investigate,
Analyze, Hypothesize, Implement --- are a formalization of what
experienced debuggers do and what inexperienced debuggers skip.
The inexperienced pattern is "see an error, try a fix, see if it
works, repeat"; this is stochastic gradient descent in the space
of code changes and it can take hours to converge on bugs that
could be fixed in minutes with a disciplined investigation.

The "3+ failed fixes → question the architecture" heuristic is
the most valuable single rule in the debugging skill. When an
agent (or a human) has tried three fixes and none has worked, the
bug is almost always not where the symptom appeared; it is in a
structural assumption that all three fixes shared. The rule forces
the agent to zoom out.

The closing sentence --- "iron laws prevent the most common and
most dangerous shortcuts" --- is the right framing. Skills do not
prevent unusual failures; unusual failures are by definition not
predictable. Skills prevent the common ones, the failures that
happen over and over, the shortcuts that are taken not because
the practitioner doesn't know better but because the shortcut is
what the moment seems to demand.

SLIDE 22 — EXAMPLE 4: MATH OLYMPIAD SKILL (THE CEILING)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    22/34
    Example 4: Math Olympiad Skill (The Ceiling)
    1.  Interpretation check — catch ambiguous problem readings
    2.  8–12 parallel solver agents (thinking only, no tools)
    3.  Strip thinking traces before verification
    4.  Fresh adversarial verifiers (blind to solver reasoning)
    5.  Asymmetric voting (4 to confirm, 2 to refute)
    6.  Revision cycles (up to 3 rounds)
    7.  Calibrated abstention — "no confident solution" rather than bluff
    8.  Presentation pass (LaTeX/PDF output)
    400+ line skill orchestrating multiple subagents. This is the ceiling,
    not the floor. Your first skill can be 20 lines.

COMMENTARY

The math-olympiad skill, originally developed by Anthropic as an
official plugin, is included as an example not because most
participants will ever write a skill like it but because it
demonstrates how far the paradigm can be pushed. Each of its eight
steps is worth describing briefly.

*Step 1. Interpretation check.* Olympiad problems are frequently
ambiguous on first reading. The skill's first step is to generate
multiple possible readings of the problem and ask the user to
confirm which is intended. This is a failure mode that human
olympiad coaches know well: the student solves the wrong problem
because they read it wrong in the first ten seconds.

*Step 2. Eight-to-twelve parallel solver agents.* The skill spawns
many subagents, each asked to solve the problem independently. They
are "thinking only, no tools" because olympiad problems are pure
reasoning and tool use is a distraction. The parallelism exploits
the stochasticity of LLM sampling: different sampling trajectories
find different approaches, and the best of many is much better than
the best of one.

*Step 3. Strip thinking traces before verification.* This is a
subtle but critical step. When the solvers' outputs are passed to
the verifiers, the verifiers must see only the final proofs, not
the solvers' thinking traces. If the verifier sees the thinking,
it anchors on the solver's reasoning and fails to catch errors. By
stripping the trace, the skill forces the verifier to reconstruct
the reasoning from scratch and catch errors the solver missed.

*Step 4. Fresh adversarial verifiers.* The verifiers are new
subagents, blind to the solver reasoning, instructed to find
errors. Adversarial framing is important because the default LLM
tendency is to agree; "find what is wrong with this proof" elicits
different behaviour from "does this proof look right".

*Step 5. Asymmetric voting (4 to confirm, 2 to refute).* It should
take more verifiers to accept a proof than to reject one. This
asymmetry is a decision-theoretic choice: the cost of accepting a
wrong proof (publishing a wrong result) is much higher than the
cost of rejecting a right one (redoing the work). The skill encodes
this cost asymmetry.

*Step 6. Revision cycles.* Up to three rounds of solver-verifier
iteration are permitted; after the third, the skill escalates to
abstention rather than continue to iterate, because the probability
of converging decreases with each round.

*Step 7. Calibrated abstention.* The skill tells the agent it is
allowed to say "no confident solution." This is the feature that
most distinguishes the skill from a naive agent: the naive agent
always produces an answer because producing an answer is what
agents do, while the skill-guided agent refuses when it cannot
justify the answer. Calibrated abstention is to olympiad math what
"no estimation without a DAG" is to causal inference.

*Step 8. Presentation pass.* The final step is a presentation pass
that renders the accepted proof as LaTeX or PDF. This is
cosmetic but important: the user receives a polished artefact, not
raw markdown.

The skill is 400+ lines long and orchestrates multiple subagents
with delicate inter-agent communication protocols. It is the
ceiling of what is currently achievable with skills, and it should
not be the model for a first skill. A first skill can be twenty
lines. The point of showing the ceiling is to reassure participants
that the paradigm scales; the point of the "your first skill can
be 20 lines" reminder is to prevent them from being intimidated
into not writing one at all.

COMMENTARY

The ecosystem slide is a snapshot of the situation as of early 2026.
Several things about that snapshot are worth noting.

First, there is convergence on a single format. The
agentskills.io specification defines a SKILL.md file with a
specific frontmatter schema, and this schema is implemented by
multiple agent runtimes. A skill written for Claude Code will also
run, with no changes, in Cursor, Kimi Code, and Gemini CLI. This
cross-platform property is the property that turns skills from a
product feature into a community standard.

Second, the ecosystem is being built by different kinds of
organizations in parallel. baygent-skills is maintained by a
Bayesian statistics education community (the authors of the
Learning Bayesian Statistics podcast). obra/superpowers is
maintained by an individual software engineer. The Anthropic
official plugins are maintained by the company that builds
Claude. Each of these communities is writing the skills their
constituency needs, and the fact that the skills are
interchangeable across runtimes means a Bayesian statistician can
adopt the superpowers TDD skill without changing agents.

Third, skills are licensed. The baygent-skills repository is MIT
licensed, which means anyone can adapt, fork, or redistribute it.
This matters because it establishes the norm: skills are shared
knowledge, not proprietary assets.

The comparison to packages is the right mental model. Python's
NumPy is not a feature of CPython; it is a package published to
PyPI under an open license, maintained by a community, versioned,
and depended on by other packages. Skills will have the same
structure: published to a registry, versioned, composable, and
depended on by other skills.

*A terminology warning.* The filename `SKILL.md` is now used by at
least two different ecosystems with different meanings, and a
participant who reads both will be briefly confused. In the Claude
Code / baygent-skills / agentskills.io lineage that this companion
treats, a SKILL.md encodes a *methodology*: steps, checkpoints,
critical rules, gotchas. In the OpenClaw / MoltBook lineage
presented at the MIT/Harvard AI Agents workshop (Imagination in
Action, April 9, 2026, the day before ours), SKILL.md is instead a
per-tool *how-to* file, "markdown instructions that teach an agent
how to use a tool or API", with dozens shipping by default in
OpenClaw's harness. Both conventions are legitimate; they are just
naming different artefacts. A skill in the first sense is closer to
a lab protocol; a skill in the second sense is closer to a man
page. If you encounter the filename in a new codebase, open it and
read the first section to determine which of the two you are
looking at.

*An honest counterargument.* Not everyone in the agents community
thinks the skills-as-methodology paradigm is necessary. Mario
Zechner, author of OpenClaw's "Pi" harness, argues for what he
calls *radical minimalism*: "All frontier models have been
RL-trained extensively --- they inherently understand what a coding
agent is." On that view, the model already knows what bash is, how
files work, and what a well-run analysis looks like, so wrapping it
in a skill with a long SKILL.md just wastes context tokens on
instructions the model would have followed anyway. Pi ships with
four tools (read, write, edit, bash), a system prompt under a
thousand tokens, and no plan mode, subagents, or MCP support by
design. The contrarian position is worth stating plainly because it
is defensible for some workloads: if your task really is generic
coding and the model's default behaviour is what you want, adding a
skill is overhead. The case for skills is strongest exactly where
the default behaviour is wrong --- where the training data is
dominated by a convention you do not want (the Bayesian workflow's
"Normal(0,1) and vibes" default, for instance), or where a
guardrail is easy to state but hard to enforce without a written
rule. In that sense the two philosophies are not opposed; they
describe different regions of the task space. Radical minimalism is
the right default for tasks the model already does well; skills are
the intervention for tasks where it does not.

SLIDE 24 — CREATING SKILLS IS JUST MARKDOWN

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    24/34
    Creating Skills — It's Just Markdown

        # Create the skill directory
        mkdir -p ~/.claude/skills/my-skill/

        # Write the SKILL.md (use any editor — or ask Claude Code!)
        # "Help me create a skill for [your workflow]"

        # Restart Claude Code to load the new skill

        /my-skill

        # Or just describe a task — Claude auto-activates matching skills
        "Help me analyze this dataset using our lab's methodology"

COMMENTARY

The mechanical process of creating a skill is startlingly simple, and
this simplicity is the design's most important feature. A skill is a
directory in `~/.claude/skills/` containing a SKILL.md file with
frontmatter. That is all. There is no build step, no registration
ritual, no database. The reason this matters is that the cost of
writing a skill must be low enough that researchers will write them
as a side effect of ordinary work; if writing a skill required
setting up a project, the paradigm would not take hold.

The restart instruction is important because Claude Code caches the
skill list at startup. If you write a new skill in a running session,
the agent will not see it until the session is restarted. This is a
small friction but a frequent source of confusion; the fix is always
"restart Claude Code".

The invocation model has two paths. The explicit path is to type
`/my-skill`, which activates the skill unconditionally. The implicit
path is to describe the task in natural language and let the agent
match the description against the skill's frontmatter description.
The implicit path is the one you want for ordinary use; it means the
user doesn't have to remember which skill applies to which task, and
the agent will pull in the right skill as soon as the task matches.
This is why the description field is so important: it is the
matching key.

The pro tip --- use `/skill-creator` --- is the meta-observation that
writing a skill is itself a task, and the task has a methodology,
and that methodology can be a skill. The skill-creator skill
interviews the user about the workflow to be encoded, writes a
draft SKILL.md, generates test scenarios, runs them against an
unscaffolded agent and a scaffolded one, and iterates. It is an
example of skills all the way down, and it is the fastest path from
"I have a workflow" to "I have a tested skill".

*A note for Harvard participants.* Session 3 configured Claude Code
to call models through the Harvard HUIT Bedrock endpoint rather than
Anthropic's public API. Skills are orthogonal to that choice: the
`~/.claude/skills/` directory lives on your local filesystem, is
read by the Claude Code client at startup, and is applied regardless
of which backend the client is talking to. Nothing in the Session 3
setup needs to be redone. A participant who set up Bedrock last week
can write a SKILL.md today and it will activate on the next
`claude` invocation.

SLIDE 25 — THINKING ABOUT YOUR RESEARCH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    25/34
    Thinking About YOUR Research
    1.  What task do you do repeatedly? (data cleaning, model fitting,
        writing, reviewing...)
    2.  What steps does your advisor/PI always remind you about?
        → Those are your critical rules.
    3.  What mistakes have you made that you wish you'd been warned about?
        → Those are your common gotchas.
    4.  What would a new lab member need to know to do this task correctly?
        → That IS your skill.

COMMENTARY

This slide is the pivotal instructional moment in the session. Every question on it is a method for eliciting skill
content from the researcher's own experience, and in the hands-on
portion each question maps directly to a section of the template.

*What task do you do repeatedly?* The test for whether a workflow
deserves a skill is repetition. A one-time analysis does not justify
the effort of writing a skill; a task you have done (or will do)
three times does. In practice, researchers underestimate how much of
their work is repeated. The exercise here is to list every distinct
technical workflow performed in the last month --- data cleaning,
model fitting, literature review, figure generation, paper
submission --- and ask which ones will be repeated. Any task on that
list with a frequency greater than "once" is a skill candidate.

*What steps does your advisor/PI always remind you about?* The
advisor's reminders are the critical rules, because an advisor's
reminders are themselves the externalization of the lab's
community knowledge. When your advisor says "don't forget to
check for multicollinearity before interpreting coefficients", that
sentence is a critical rule verbatim. The exercise is to recall the
last three reminders you received and write them down.

*What mistakes have you made that you wish you'd been warned
about?* The mistakes are the common gotchas. A gotcha is
distinguished from a rule in that it describes a specific failure
and the specific correction; "pandas.read_csv silently parses
dates as strings unless parse_dates is set, which will cause your
time-series plots to look monotonic-in-filename order" is a
gotcha because it describes the symptom, the cause, and the fix.
The exercise is to recall the last three bugs that took more than
an hour to debug and convert them into gotcha entries.

*What would a new lab member need to know to do this task
correctly?* This is the aggregation question and it is the one
that, when answered honestly, produces the skill. The exercise is
to imagine a first-year graduate student joining the lab tomorrow
and being handed this task. What would you want them to read
before they started? The answer is the skill.

These four questions are designed to be completed in five minutes.
Participants in the workshop were asked to answer them in writing
before proceeding to the template.

SLIDE 26 — EXAMPLE: LITERATURE REVIEW SKILL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    26/34
    Example: Literature Review Skill

    ---
    name: literature-review
    description: >
      Systematic literature review workflow. Use when asked to review
      literature, find related work, or prepare a related work section.
    ---

    # Literature Review

    ## Workflow
    1. **Define scope** -- research question, inclusion/exclusion  ⚠️ CONFIRM
    2. **Search strategy** -- key terms, databases, search strings
    3. **Screen results** -- extract, classify, note key findings
    4. **Synthesize themes** -- group by theme, identify gaps
    5. **Critical assessment** -- design strengths, biases, limits
    6. **Write summary** -- landscape → themes → gaps → references

    ## Critical rules
    - Never fabricate citations. If uncertain, say so explicitly.
    - Always note the search date.
    - Distinguish claims from evidence.

COMMENTARY

The literature review skill is a deliberately small example. It has
six workflow steps, three critical rules, and zero gotchas, and it
fits on a single slide. It is included to show what a first skill
can look like, and to normalize the smallness.

Each design choice is worth a brief comment.

*Scope with confirm.* The first step has a ⚠️ CONFIRM marker
because scope creep is the most common failure of literature
reviews. An agent asked to "review the literature on X" will
often return results on X, X-adjacent, and a tangent that was more
interesting than X. The confirmation forces the agent to state the
scope in writing and have the user approve it before any searching
begins.

*Search strategy as a discrete step.* The search strategy is not
the search itself; it is the declaration of key terms, databases,
and search strings that will be used. This is the step that makes
the review reproducible. Without this step, "how did you find
these papers" has no answer.

*Screen, synthesize, assess as separate steps.* Screening is
mechanical (did this paper make it through the inclusion criteria);
synthesis is thematic (what do the included papers tell us
together); critical assessment is evaluative (are the included
papers any good). Conflating these three is the most common error
in novice literature reviews, and the skill separates them so the
agent cannot conflate them either.

*Write summary with a specific structure.* The "landscape → themes
→ gaps → references" ordering is the convention in most scientific
literature review sections and it gives the agent a target shape
for the output.

*Critical rule: never fabricate citations.* This is the iron law.
LLM-written literature reviews are notorious for fabricated
citations; a skill that forbids fabrication --- and, more
importantly, instructs the agent to explicitly note uncertainty
when it is unsure --- eliminates the failure mode. In practice this
rule must be enforced with a secondary verification step (checking
each cited DOI or arXiv ID is resolvable), and a robust skill would
include that step explicitly.

*Critical rule: always note the search date.* Search results are
time-dependent; a review that does not note when the search was
performed cannot be updated or audited.

*Critical rule: distinguish claims from evidence.* A paper's
claim ("method X improves accuracy") and the evidence for it
("Table 3 shows a 2% improvement on benchmark Y") are different
objects. The skill forbids conflating them.

*Related tools: deep research.* The literature-review skill
automates the *structure* of a review --- scope, screen,
synthesize, assess, write --- but the search phase itself is best
served by dedicated "deep research" tools that crawl, read, and
summarize literature on their own. As of April 2026 the main
options a Harvard researcher will encounter, in alphabetical order,
are:

- *Elicit* (elicit.com) --- academic-paper-first, optimized for
  systematic-review workflows and extraction tables.
- *Gemini Deep Research* (inside Gemini Advanced) --- Google's
  agentic research mode; good integration with Google Scholar and
  with NotebookLM.
- *NotebookLM* (notebooklm.google.com) --- the tool Session 1 of
  this series already introduced; strongest when you supply the
  source documents yourself and want grounded synthesis.
- *OpenAI Deep Research* (inside ChatGPT) --- agentic literature
  search producing citation-rich long-form reports; strong on
  breadth, weaker on paywalled content.
- *Perplexity Deep Research* (perplexity.ai) --- web-first with
  inline citations; fast and broad, but grounding varies by source
  quality.

None of these replaces the literature-review skill; they *feed*
it. A realistic workflow is: run one of the deep research tools to
get a first pass of candidate papers, then invoke the
literature-review skill in Claude Code to screen, synthesize, and
write the section under the skill's discipline. The skill's "never
fabricate citations" rule applies with particular force here: deep
research tools can and do surface papers that do not exist, and the
verification step must be part of the skill's workflow, not an
afterthought.

SLIDE 27 — EXAMPLE: DATA ANALYSIS SKILL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    27/34
    Example: Data Analysis Skill

    ---
    name: data-analysis
    description: >
      Structured data analysis workflow for quantitative research.
      Use when asked to analyze a dataset or build statistical models.
    ---

    # Data Analysis Workflow

    ## Workflow
    1. **Understand** -- data dictionary, types, units, missingness
    2. **Explore** -- summary stats, distributions, correlations, outliers
    3. **Hypothesize** -- write expectations before modeling  ⚠️ CONFIRM
    4. **Specify model** -- choose family, document rationale
    5. **Fit + diagnose** -- residuals, influence, VIF
    6. **Validate** -- hold-out or cross-validation
    7. **Visualize** -- publication-quality, labeled, sourced
    8. **Report** -- effect sizes + CIs, not just p-values

    ## Critical rules
    - Never drop observations without documenting why.
    - Always check assumptions before interpreting.
    - Save intermediates to disk — don't rely on notebook state.

COMMENTARY

The data analysis skill is slightly larger than the literature review
skill --- eight workflow steps, three critical rules --- and it is
the skill most participants will find closest to their own work.

*Understand before explore.* Step 1 is reading the data
dictionary, checking types, verifying units, and measuring
missingness. This is before any summary statistics are computed,
because summary statistics on mis-typed data are meaningless, and
units that are off by a factor of 1000 will invalidate every
downstream step. "Understand" is the intellectual equivalent of
washing your hands before surgery.

*Explore with a specific agenda.* "Summary stats, distributions,
correlations, outliers" is a specific list, not a vague
instruction. The skill tells the agent what to explore, because
the default behavior of an unconstrained agent is to explore only
the first two columns and move on.

*Hypothesize with confirmation.* The third step is the most
distinctive. Before any model is specified, the skill asks the
agent to write down what the agent *expects* the data to show
--- and to confirm this expectation with the user. This is the
data analysis equivalent of pre-registration: it creates a record
of the a priori belief, against which the posterior result can be
compared. When the result matches the expectation, the conclusion
is strengthened; when it does not, the analyst is forced to
explain the discrepancy rather than pretending the unexpected
result was always what they were looking for.

*Specify, fit-and-diagnose, validate, visualize, report.* The
remaining five steps are the conventional arc of a statistical
analysis, with specific diagnostic requirements: VIF for
collinearity, residuals for model fit, influence measures for
outlier leverage, holdout for out-of-sample generalization, effect
sizes and confidence intervals rather than p-values for
reporting. Each of these is a place where the unconstrained agent
will take a shortcut.

*Critical rule: never drop observations without documenting why.*
Quiet dropping of observations is the fastest way to introduce
unintentional bias. The rule does not forbid dropping; it
requires documentation. A skill cannot prevent the analyst from
making a bad decision, but it can ensure the bad decision is
visible.

*Critical rule: always check assumptions before interpreting.*
Assumption checking is the step that determines whether the
interpretation is meaningful. The rule forces the check to come
first.

*Critical rule: save intermediates to disk.* Notebook state is
the enemy of reproducibility. An analysis that depends on the
order in which cells were executed is not an analysis; it is a
one-time event. The rule forces the analysis to be a function of
files on disk.

*A note on format.* Sessions 2 and 3 of this series were notebook-

the agent's behavior with and without a skill. Participants are
encouraged to pick whichever track they find more concrete, and
Track B in particular leans on material Andorra has already

COMMENTARY

The five-fifteen-five time budget is empirical: in previous
workshops, participants who spent less than five minutes on Step 1
(choosing the workflow) ended up with poorly-scoped skills, and
participants who spent more than fifteen minutes on Step 2 (writing
the SKILL.md) ran out of time for testing, which is the step that
catches the mistakes.

The second invocation path --- "let Claude help write it" --- is
the faster one and it is what most participants should use in
practice. The reason is that writing good markdown is itself a
skill (of the human kind), and delegating the drafting to the agent
means the participant can focus on the *content* of the skill (what
the steps are) rather than the *form* (how to structure the
frontmatter). The conversational pattern is: the human types the
answers to the four questions from Slide 25, the agent drafts a
SKILL.md based on those answers, the human reviews and revises.

The test step at the end is the most skipped and the most
important. Testing means: restart Claude Code, invoke the skill
with a realistic prompt, and watch whether the agent actually
follows the workflow. If the agent follows the workflow, the skill
works; if the agent skips a step or misinterprets a rule, the
skill needs revision. The failure mode to watch for is "the agent
acknowledged the skill but didn't apply it", which is almost
always caused by an under-specified description or too-weak rule
language.

SLIDE 30 — THE TEMPLATE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    30/34
    The Template

    ---
    name: my-skill
    description: >
      [What does this do? What phrases should trigger it?]
    ---

    # [Skill Name]

    ## Workflow
    1. **[Think]** -- [What question/setup first?]
    2. **[Think]** -- [What assumptions to state?]  ⚠️ CONFIRM
    3. **[Do]** -- [First implementation step]
    4. **[Validate]** -- [What check must pass?]
    5. **[Do]** -- [Next implementation step]
    6. **[Report]** -- [How should results look?]

    ## Critical rules
    - [What must the agent ALWAYS do?]
    - [What must the agent NEVER do?]

    ## Common gotchas
    - [What goes wrong] → [How to fix it]

    Domains: EDA · hypothesis testing · experiment design · data cleaning ·
    ML eval · time series · lit review

COMMENTARY

The template on this slide is the one every participant copies into
their first SKILL.md. It has three deliberately unusual features.

First, the workflow is organized as Think-Think-Do-Validate-Do-
Report. The doubled "Think" steps at the start are to emphasize
that the first two phases are reasoning, not computation. The
Validate step in the middle is the checkpoint. The Report at the
end is the artefact. This shape --- think first, do carefully,
validate explicitly, report clearly --- generalizes to almost every
quantitative research task.

Second, the critical rules section has exactly two bullets. Not
three, not five, not fifteen. The template uses two because the
author's experience is that novice skill writers tend to cram too
many rules into the first draft, and then the agent cannot
distinguish the important rules from the merely preferred ones.
Two is enough to get started; more can be added after the first
round of testing reveals which additional rules are needed.

Third, the gotchas section is structured as arrow statements:
what-goes-wrong → how-to-fix. The arrow syntax is not required by
the spec but it is recommended because it forces the author to
think in problem-solution pairs, which is the most useful form for
gotcha knowledge.

The domains list at the bottom of the slide is a suggestion
engine: participants who cannot think of a workflow to encode can
pick one from the list. In previous workshops, the most popular
choices have been EDA (exploratory data analysis), data cleaning,
and literature review.

SLIDE 31 — TRACK B: INSTALL AND TEST EXISTING SKILLS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    31/34
    Track B: Install & Test Existing Skills

        # Option 1: Bayesian workflow skills
        git clone https://github.com/Learning-Bayesian-Statistics/baygent-skills /tmp/baygent
        mkdir -p ~/.claude/skills
        cp -r /tmp/baygent/bayesian-workflow ~/.claude/skills/
        cp -r /tmp/baygent/causal-inference ~/.claude/skills/

        # Option 2: Software engineering skills
        git clone https://github.com/obra/superpowers /tmp/superpowers
        cp -r /tmp/superpowers/skills/* ~/.claude/skills/

        # Then restart Claude Code and try:
        "Build a simple Bayesian regression model for this data"

        # Watch: does it follow the 10-step workflow?
        # Now remove the skill and try the same prompt:
        # What steps does it skip?

    The difference is dramatic. Skills turn a generic assistant into a
    methodology-following expert.

COMMENTARY

Track B is the learn-by-comparison track. The participant installs
an existing skill, runs a task, observes the output, removes the
skill, runs the same task, and observes the difference. The
comparison is what makes the value of skills visible; until a
participant has seen the same prompt produce two different workflows
--- one disciplined, one not --- the argument for skills is
abstract.

The prompt to use for the comparison is important. It should be a
prompt squarely in the skill's domain ("build a Bayesian regression
model" for the bayesian-workflow skill) but generic enough that an
unscaffolded agent will produce output without complaining. The goal is to elicit the agent's default behavior
and compare it to the skill-guided behavior, step by step.

What to look for in the comparison, in order of importance: (1)
which steps does the unscaffolded agent skip entirely (typically
prior predictive checks, prior sensitivity, and LOO-PIT
calibration); (2) which steps does the unscaffolded agent perform
but superficially (typically convergence diagnostics, where the
agent checks R-hat but not ESS or E-BFMI); (3) which steps does
the unscaffolded agent perform adequately but in a non-standard
way (typically reporting, where the agent uses frequentist
language or a 95% confidence interval when the field convention
is a 94% credible interval).

The three differences above are the three places where the skill
adds value. A participant who sees all three in one comparison has
understood the paragraph "why skills matter" at a level no slide
can convey.

*Using Andorra's published eval material as your exercise inputs.*
Alex Andorra did not write a formal workshop tutorial for baygent-
skills, but he published something better: a complete evaluation
harness that a participant can use as ready-made exercise material.
Inside the cloned repository, look at:

    baygent-skills/
    └── evals/
        └── bayesian-workflow/
            ├── trigger_eval_set.json      (21 activation queries:
            │                                10 should-trigger,
            │                                11 should-not-trigger)
            └── iteration-1/
                ├── benchmark.json          (the reproducible eval)
                ├── count-data-overdispersion/
                ├── hierarchical-schools/
                ├── logistic-regression-churn/
                ├── model-comparison-houses/
                ├── prior-elicitation-report/
                ├── prior-sensitivity-check/
                └── troubleshooting-divergences/

The benchmark.json file aggregates six of these seven scenarios
(prior-sensitivity-check is included in the directory but is not
part of the main benchmark run). Across the six aggregated
scenarios, the skill-armed agent passes 53 of 53 individual
assertion checks (100%) and the unscaffolded agent passes 48 of 53
(90.6%, which the slide rounds to 90.5%). The wall-clock time and
token-usage costs from the same benchmark file are +29.1% and
+87.2% respectively, matching the slide's "+29% time, +87% tokens"
to within rounding.

There are three concrete exercises you can run with these files,
each taking five to ten minutes, none requiring that you invent
your own prompt:

*Exercise B1 (two minutes --- does the skill activate?).* Open
`evals/bayesian-workflow/trigger_eval_set.json`. It is a list of
twenty-one research-flavoured user prompts with a
`"should_trigger": true/false` label on each (ten should trigger
the skill, eleven should not). Pick two from the `should_trigger:
true` half and one from the `should_trigger: false` half. Paste each into Claude Code. The skill should activate
on the first two and stay silent on the third. If it does not, the
description field in SKILL.md is mis-specified for your wording ---
and this is the single most common way a skill fails in practice.
Sample triggering prompts from the file, verbatim:

    "I've got survey data from 3000 respondents across 12 regions
    and I want to estimate the probability that someone supports a
    policy change, accounting for regional variation. Some regions
    only have 30 respondents so I need the estimates to borrow
    strength. Can you build a Bayesian model for this?"

    "my pymc model has like 200 divergences and rhat is 1.05 on
    the variance parameter. the trace plot for chain 3 looks
    totally flat. what's going wrong and how do i fix it?"

    "I'm trying to understand the effect of temperature on crop
    yield using data from 50 farms over 10 years. I want to
    account for the fact that farms might respond differently to
    temperature. Should I use a hierarchical model?"

Sample non-triggering prompts (the skill should stay silent) that
Andorra included to test specificity:

    "I need to run an A/B test analysis for our website redesign.
    ... Can you calculate the p-value and tell me if the difference
    is statistically significant?"

    "I want to forecast daily sales for the next 90 days using
    Prophet."

If you run all three against the installed skill and the silent-
case stays silent, you have verified that Andorra's description
field discriminates Bayesian questions from adjacent-but-unrelated
quantitative questions. If the silent case triggers anyway, you
have a live demonstration of a description writing failure and a
concrete target to fix.

*Exercise B2 (five to ten minutes --- run a real benchmark
scenario).* Pick one of the seven scenario directories in
`iteration-1/` --- `logistic-regression-churn` is the most
approachable, since churn prediction is familiar and the rubric
has ten specific checks. Read the scenario's prompt, paste it into
Claude Code with the skill active, and watch the agent work. Then
open `iteration-1/benchmark.json` and look at the run for
`logistic-regression-churn` / `configuration: with_skill`: the
`expectations` field is a list of ten pass/fail checks like:

    "Uses Bernoulli likelihood (pm.Bernoulli) with logit inverse-link
     function"
    "Standardizes or scales predictors before fitting the model"
    "Sets weakly informative priors on regression coefficients
     (Normal with sigma between 1 and 5)"
    "Calls pm.sample_prior_predictive() before inference"
    "Reports 94% HDI (not 95% CI) for parameter estimates"
    "Uses coords and dims in the PyMC model definition"

Each check has an `evidence` field quoting the line of code that
satisfied it. Use the expectations as your own rubric: grade your
agent's output against the same ten checks, and note where it
passes, where it fails, and whether your failures are the same
ones Andorra's without-skill run caught.

*Exercise B3 (advanced, for participants who finish B1 and B2
early).* Write your own scenario in the same format as
`iteration-1/logistic-regression-churn/`: a prompt from your own
research area, ten expectations the agent's output must satisfy,
and a name. Run it against the installed skill. You have just
written an evaluation for someone else's skill using your own
domain knowledge --- which is the most valuable thing a researcher
can contribute to an open skill project. If the skill fails on
your scenario, the failure is a real contribution back to
baygent-skills; open an issue on the repo with the scenario file
and the agent's output.

What makes these exercises work as workshop material is that they
are Andorra's own benchmark, unmodified. You are not inventing a
test; you are reproducing a test an author has already validated.
The 90.5% / 100% numbers on Slide 18 come directly from the file
you are grading against. That provides a ground truth that pure
"try a prompt and see what happens" exercises lack.

questions cover the three sections of the SKILL.md, and a good
answer to all three implies a well-rounded skill.

SLIDE 33 — KEY TAKEAWAYS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE CONTENT (verbatim)
    33/34
    Key Takeaways
    1.  A skill is a markdown file that teaches an agent your methodology
    2.  Skills encode domain expertise, enforce guardrails, prevent common
        mistakes
    3.  Anatomy: frontmatter (name + description) + steps + rules + gotchas
    4.  Start simple: 3–8 steps, 1–3 rules, 1–2 gotchas — refine over time
    5.  Share your skills: reusable, composable, platform-portable
    "Slow productivity" — skills force thinking before coding.
    The value is in the guardrails: the things you would otherwise forget
    under time pressure.

COMMENTARY

The five takeaways are the session's thesis in compressed form.

*1. A skill is a markdown file that teaches an agent your
methodology.* This is the definition. Not a prompt, not a function,
not a plugin. A markdown file. The simplicity of the medium is the
paradigm's most important property.

*2. Skills encode domain expertise, enforce guardrails, prevent
common mistakes.* These are the three functions a skill performs.
Encoding is the easy part (you write down what you know); enforcing
is where the ⚠️ CONFIRM markers do their work; preventing is where
the critical rules and gotchas matter.

*3. Anatomy: frontmatter (name + description) + steps + rules +
gotchas.* The structure is standard. A skill that departs from this
structure is almost always a skill that is harder to maintain and
harder for agents to follow.

*4. Start simple: 3–8 steps, 1–3 rules, 1–2 gotchas — refine over
time.* This is the most practical advice on the slide. The first
skill should be small. Small skills are easier to test, easier to
debug, and easier to finish. A finished small skill is strictly
more useful than an unfinished large one.

*5. Share your skills: reusable, composable, platform-portable.*
Skills are a community artefact. A skill you write for your own use
is usable by your labmates, by your field, and by the broader
research community. The cost of sharing is a git push; the benefit
is distributed expertise.

The closing quote is from Cal Newport's notion of "slow
productivity", borrowed here because the paradigm fits. Skills
force thinking before coding. In an era when LLM-assisted coding
can produce working code in seconds, the bottleneck is no longer
typing; it is deciding what to type. Skills are the artefact that
encodes the deciding.

The final sentence of the slide --- "the value is in the guardrails:
the things you would otherwise forget under time pressure" --- is
the session's final claim. The guardrails are not the boring part
of a skill; they are the point. The agent remembers them so you
don't have to.

================================================================================
                      APPENDIX B.  GLOSSARY OF TERMS
================================================================================

Agent. A program that uses an LLM as a planner, calls tools, observes
the results, and iterates until a goal is reached.

Agent loop. The Think-Act-Observe-Decide while loop at the core of
every modern coding agent.

CLAUDE.md. A markdown file loaded automatically into every Claude Code
session in a given directory. Used for always-active instructions
(conventions, preferences) that apply to all tasks in the project.

Checkpoint. A mandatory step in a skill workflow at which the agent
must verify progress, typically by confirming with the user or by
running a diagnostic whose output must pass before proceeding. Marked
in SKILL.md with a ⚠️ CONFIRM or equivalent symbol.

Critical rule. A non-negotiable guardrail in a skill, stated as an
"always" or "never" statement, enforced by the agent even under
time pressure.

Frontmatter. The YAML block at the top of a SKILL.md file containing
the skill's name and description. The name is the identifier; the
description determines when the skill is activated.

Gotcha. An entry in a skill's gotchas section describing a specific
failure mode, the symptom it produces, and the correction.

MCP (Model Context Protocol). A cross-vendor protocol for giving
agents access to external tools. Complementary to skills: MCP expands
what actions the agent can take; skills expand what workflows the
agent can follow.

Progressive disclosure. The design pattern in which a skill's SKILL.md
is kept short and pointed, while deeper material is loaded on demand
from the skill's references/ directory.

Refutation test. A diagnostic in causal inference whose purpose is to
falsify an estimate if the underlying assumptions are wrong. Placebo
outcomes, negative controls, and unobserved confounder bounds are
examples. Mandatory in the causal inference skill.

Skill. A structured markdown document, typically stored in
~/.claude/skills/, that teaches an agent a domain workflow. A skill
consists of frontmatter, a workflow section, critical rules, and
common gotchas.

Subagent. A new agent loop spawned by a parent agent, with its own
context, tools, and goal. Subagents enable delegation, parallelism,
and isolation.

Workflow. A predefined sequence of steps in which the control flow is
fixed and deterministic. Contrasted with an agent, in which the
control flow is LLM-driven.

================================================================================
            APPENDIX E.  LIMITATIONS OF THIS COMPANION DOCUMENT
================================================================================

A companion document is not a primary source. The following are the
limitations a reader should keep in mind.

1. *Not an experimental report.* No evaluation was run for this
   companion. Quantitative claims originate in Alex Andorra's two
   learnbayesstats essays (2026-03-16 and 2026-03-23) and in the
   baygent-skills repository at v1.2. These primary sources were
   consulted and the numbers were reconciled against them in
   Appendix D, but the underlying evaluation protocol (evaluator
   model version, scoring rubric, random seeds, confidence intervals)
   is as reported by Andorra and was not independently replicated.

2. *Opinionated commentary.* The commentary under each slide is the
   author's interpretation and framing, written to help a reader
   understand the slide's argument. For the Bayesian workflow and
   stress-test slides, the commentary has been reconciled against
   Andorra's primary sources; for the causal inference, software
   engineering, and math olympiad slides, the commentary rests on
   the slide content plus this companion author's reading of the
   surrounding literature. Where the commentary goes beyond the
   primary source, it is the companion author's framing, not a claim
   the original skill authors made.

3. *Snapshot in time.* The skills ecosystem is evolving. URLs,
   repository layouts, default sampler choices, R-hat thresholds, and
   even the frontmatter schema of SKILL.md may change after the date
   on the title page. Readers encountering a discrepancy between this
   companion and a current source should trust the current source.

4. *Single-voice editorial framing.* The narrative argues for skills
   as the solution to a specific problem of agent indiscipline. This
   framing is defensible but not neutral; a reader who wants a
   contrarian view should read the "Building Effective Agents" essay,
   which discusses when NOT to reach for an agentic approach at all.

5. *Unverified third-party links.* URLs reproduced from the slides
   (agentskills.io, the arXiv link on Slide 34, the workshop materials
   link on Slide 34) were not verified in the preparation of this
   companion. Readers should expect some dead or drifted links.

6. *Cited empirical effects are from small N.* As noted in Appendix D,
   the Bayesian and causal evaluations that anchor Slides 18 and 20
   are six-scenario samples. The effect sizes are suggestive but the
   precision of the point estimates is low. A fair summary is that
   skill-guided agents outperform unscaffolded agents on these
   benchmarks by a margin larger than sampling noise can plausibly
   explain, with the exact magnitude uncertain.
